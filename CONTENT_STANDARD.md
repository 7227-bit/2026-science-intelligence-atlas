# Content Standard

## Purpose

Atlas content converts scientific and technological developments into structured, evidence-backed intelligence. Every record must help a non-specialist understand both the event and the system around it without overstating what the evidence supports.

## Editorial principles

### Accuracy and provenance

- Never invent facts or sources.
- Verify names, dates, metrics, quotations, and claims against cited material.
- Prefer primary evidence such as papers, official datasets, regulatory records, institutional releases, filings, and technical documentation.
- Use high-quality secondary sources for context and independent interpretation.
- Link to the most direct stable source available.

### Evidence versus inference

Use explicit framing:

- **Evidence:** what a source directly reports, demonstrates, measures, or documents.
- **Inference:** an interpretation derived from evidence; explain the reasoning.
- **Forecast:** a future-oriented judgment; identify assumptions and indicators.
- **Scenario:** a plausible outcome, not a prediction; state the conditions required.

Do not blur these modes within a sentence. Avoid certainty words such as “will,” “proves,” or “solves” unless the evidence genuinely warrants them.

### Accessibility of language

- Define specialized terms on first use.
- Explain why a metric matters, not only its value.
- Use concrete causal language instead of hype.
- Prefer short paragraphs, descriptive headings, and ordered sequences.
- Retain necessary scientific nuance, uncertainty, and limitations.

## Required intelligence dossier schema

Every dossier must include all fields below. If reliable information is unavailable, state that it is unknown or not yet established rather than filling the gap with speculation.

| Field | Standard |
| --- | --- |
| Unique ID | Stable, human-readable identifier using a documented category prefix and number |
| Title | Specific, concise, and descriptive; avoid clickbait |
| Event or publication date | Date of the underlying event or publication, not merely the date an article was discovered |
| Category | One primary Atlas category; cross-field links belong in related technologies |
| Signal status | Controlled evidence-status label defined below |
| Impact score | Scored with a consistent rubric and accompanied by rationale |
| Commercial horizon | Evidence-based time range with assumptions or “not established” |
| What happened | Factual summary of the event, result, or publication |
| Why it matters | Strategic and scientific significance, including relevant second-order effects |
| Ranked bottlenecks | Ordered constraints with the basis for ranking made clear |
| Opportunity layers | Enabling capabilities, products, infrastructure, services, or markets that may benefit |
| Related technologies | Explicit cross-field dependencies and adjacent technologies |
| Major ecosystem players | Relevant organizations with their roles; inclusion is not endorsement |
| Unlock sequence | Ordered dependencies from current state toward scaled impact |
| Future milestones | Observable events that would validate, weaken, or change the outlook |
| Strategic takeaway | Concise synthesis of what decision-makers should understand |
| Sources | Traceable citations supporting the factual claims and analytical basis |

## Controlled vocabulary

### Categories

- Space
- Quantum
- Energy
- Biotechnology
- Materials
- AI and Robotics
- Climate
- Advanced Manufacturing

### Signal status

Use a small, documented set consistently. Recommended labels are:

- **Confirmed** — supported by authoritative documentation or independently established evidence.
- **Demonstrated** — a capability or result has been shown under stated conditions, but generalization or scale may remain unresolved.
- **Preliminary** — early evidence that requires replication, validation, or further data.
- **Projected** — a planned or modeled outcome that has not yet occurred.
- **Disputed** — material credible disagreement exists about the result or its interpretation.

Status describes evidence maturity, not investment quality or importance.

## Field guidance

### Impact score

Use a consistent scale across categories. The score should consider magnitude, reach across fields, enabling effects, evidence strength, and plausible duration. Record the rationale; do not imply false precision. A score may change when evidence changes.

### Commercial horizon

Distinguish technical readiness from broad commercial adoption. State key dependencies, regulatory constraints, capital requirements, and infrastructure needs that shape the estimate.

### Ranked bottlenecks

Rank constraints by their effect on progress, not by novelty. Identify whether each is primarily scientific, engineering, manufacturing, supply-chain, economic, regulatory, infrastructure, workforce, or adoption-related.

### Opportunity layers

Describe enabling layers before naming beneficiaries. Separate evidence of an active market from inferred or scenario-based opportunity. Avoid investment recommendations.

### Ecosystem players

Include organizations because their role is material and sourceable. State the role: researcher, manufacturer, operator, funder, regulator, customer, standards body, or infrastructure provider. Avoid unsupported claims of leadership.

### Unlock sequence

Express dependencies in causal order. A useful sequence answers: what must work first, what becomes possible next, and what conditions enable scale?

### Future milestones

Milestones must be observable and falsifiable where possible: a replication, trial result, commissioning, certification, cost threshold, production target, deployment, or regulatory decision. Include dates only when supported by a source and distinguish schedules from completed events.

### Strategic takeaway

Conclude with the durable insight, not a restatement of the headline. State the key constraint or dependency that governs the outlook.

## Source standard

- Cite every material factual claim close enough to trace it.
- Store source title, publisher or institution, publication date, URL, and access date where the data model permits.
- Identify preprints and non-peer-reviewed material clearly.
- Distinguish organizational claims from independent validation.
- Avoid citing search-result pages or untraceable summaries.
- Correct broken or superseded links when records are updated.
- Do not use a citation to support a stronger claim than the source makes.

## Quality review checklist

- [ ] All required dossier fields are present.
- [ ] The title and date describe the underlying event accurately.
- [ ] Scientific facts and sources are verified.
- [ ] Evidence, inference, forecasts, and scenarios are clearly separated.
- [ ] Signal status matches the maturity of evidence.
- [ ] Scores, rankings, and horizons include a defensible rationale.
- [ ] Bottlenecks and unlock sequence are causal and specific.
- [ ] Cross-field relationships are explicit.
- [ ] Language is understandable to a non-specialist.
- [ ] Uncertainty and limitations are preserved.
- [ ] Sources are direct, traceable, and sufficient for the claims.
